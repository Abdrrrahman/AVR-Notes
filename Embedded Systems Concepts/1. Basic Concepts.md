# The Three Pillars of Any Computer System

> **Definition:** Any computer system, no matter how big or small, must be built from **three essential components**: the **processor**, the **memory**, and the **input/output (I/O)**.

This holds whether we're talking about the laptop recording this lecture or a tiny chip inside a washing machine. Every computer system reduces to these three building blocks working together, keep this triad in mind, since MCU and SoC definitions later are really just variations on it.

---
# General-Purpose vs. Special-Purpose Systems

- **General-purpose:** a system like the laptop we're using right now, flexible, not restricted to one job (browsing, coding, gaming, etc.).
- **Special-purpose:** a system built for a **single, dedicated function**. Any household appliance around you - the fridge, the washing machine - is designed to do exactly one job and nothing else. This is the seed of the "embedded system" concept, since embedded systems are essentially special-purpose systems.

Whenever you design one of these, you're always working under real-world limits - mainly **power**, **cost**, and **time/flexibility** - but the design itself is what we'll spend the rest of this course on.

---
# Building a System: System Board vs. System-on-Chip
There are two ways to physically assemble a system:
## System Board
You buy every component **separately** - the microprocessor on its own, the memory on its own, the I/O on its own - and wire them together yourself on a board.
## System-on-Chip (SoC)
You buy the **entire system as one package** - processor, memory, and I/O already fused together internally on a single chip.

A quick note on what that chip physically is: an IC you hold in your hand is sealed and encapsulated. The actual silicon die is only around **10% of the package size**; the rest is **epoxy**, a protective material molded around the die at the factory.

## Comparing the Two

| Criterion           | System Board                                                                          | System-on-Chip (SoC)                                        |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Size**            | Larger                                                                                | Smaller ✅                                                   |
| **Cost**            | More expensive — each separately bought part carries its own vendor markup            | Cheaper ✅ — bought once, as one combined unit               |
| **Power**           | Higher — components are connected with external wires/traces, and wires consume power | Lower ✅ — everything is connected internally inside the die |
| **Configurability** | High ✅ — components can be swapped or upgraded (e.g. adding memory while developing)  | Low — fixed once manufactured                               |

**When to use which:** a System Board makes sense during **early development/prototyping**, when you're still experimenting with different memory or processor combinations and need that configurability. An SoC is the right choice once the design is validated and you're moving to a final, mass-produced product.

# The Integrated Circuit (IC)
>  A single chip packaging a set of circuitry into one enclosed unit, built to perform a specific, defined function.

Classic lab examples: the **555 Timer IC** or an op-amp. Depending on what circuitry sits inside, you wire the IC into a larger circuit - say, a 555 Timer with a few resistors and capacitors - to make it perform that specific function.

---
# VLSI and Moore's Law
Chips used to hold relatively little circuitry, until **VLSI** (Very Large Scale Integration) came along - still an IC in essence, but now packing **millions of transistors** onto the same die area. This gives two major benefits: a smaller physical footprint, and enormous capability, since you now have millions of transistors to build whatever logic you need.

> **Moore's Law:** the number of transistors on an IC, within the same chip area, **doubles roughly every two years** - a prediction from Gordon Moore that turned out to be historically accurate.

At this point it's worth noting: a microprocessor, a microcontroller, a System-on-Chip, and RAM are all, fundamentally, **ICs** - what differs is their internal architecture and function.

# Microprocessor vs. Microcontroller vs. MCU vs. SoC

## A Short History
Early computers were built from **vacuum tubes**, with each tube standing in for a single functional unit, picture how large a single tube is, and imagine a whole computer built that way. As the industry moved to the **transistor**, this new generation of processors became what we call the **microprocessor**. Since millions of transistors can now fit on a tiny IC, this new class of processor earned the name "micro."
## The Microprocessor (µP)
Internally, a microprocessor contains sub-components like the **ALU** (Arithmetic Logic Unit), the **Control Unit**, and **register files** - all working together to process instructions. These get covered in detail later in the course.
## Primary and Secondary Processors: CPU, GPU, DSP
The microprocessor typically acts as the **primary** processor - the master, i.e. your **CPU**. Anything else in the system, like a **GPU** or a **DSP**, acts as a **secondary** processor. A system might have just one processor (simply your CPU), or several, in which case whichever one is the master becomes the CPU while the rest support it.

A **DSP** specifically handles complex mathematical operations, offloading that heavy math work from the primary microprocessor.
## The MCU (Microcontroller Unit)
- **MPU (Microprocessor Unit)**: the microprocessor described above.
- **MCU (Microcontroller Unit)**: essentially a **small computer**: a chip containing all three pillars (processor, memory, I/O) integrated together as one unit.
## High-Performance MCU = System-on-Chip
Take the primary microprocessor, add a GPU next to it, add a DSP next to it, and fuse all of it onto one chip that combination is what we call a **System-on-Chip (SoC)**. In other words, an SoC is essentially a high-performance MCU that also bundles in secondary processors like GPU and DSP.
## Cores
The processor itself can internally contain a **single core**, **dual core**, **quad core**, or **octa core** — the number of independent processing cores packed into that one chip.

# Firmware vs. Operating-System-Based Applications
Two types of applications exist:
- **Firmware**: code that talks **directly to the hardware**, with no operating system layer in between. This is what we'll be writing in this course.
- **OS-based applications**: the application talks to the **kernel**, the kernel talks to the **drivers**, and the drivers interface with the hardware. This whole stack is outside the scope of this course, though you're welcome to study it separately.

> **Our focus is firmware**, because we'll be working directly with high-end microcontrollers — contrast this with something like Android, which relies on a full OS stack instead.

# From Silicon to Product: IC → Board → Kit → Device
1. Start with the **chip** itself: the SoC/MCU.
2. Mount it onto a **board** with a few lightweight supporting components (LEDs, etc.).
3. That combination is a **Kit**: the **Arduino Kit** is a good example, taking a microcontroller and mounting it with supporting circuitry into something a developer can plug straight into.
4. Keep building on top of the kit, adding more subsystems.
5. Stack enough of these together and you eventually get something as complex as a **car** — built, at its foundation, from many smaller chips, boards, and kits combined into one large system.
